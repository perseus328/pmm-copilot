# Positioning Framework Reference

## April Dunford "Obviously Awesome" — Complete Framework

### Overview

Positioning is not a tagline. It's the context you set in the buyer's mind before they evaluate you. Bad positioning makes great products invisible. Good positioning makes average products compelling.

The five components must be done IN ORDER — each one constrains the next.

---

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives

**The question**: What would customers do if this product didn't exist?

Three types of alternatives:
1. **Direct competitors** — named companies solving the same problem differently
2. **Indirect competitors** — different approach to the same job (e.g., hiring a consultant instead of buying software)
3. **Status quo** — do nothing, use spreadsheets, manual process, ignore the problem

**Why it matters**: Your differentiation is only meaningful relative to the alternatives your buyers are actually considering — not the ones you wish they were considering.

**Research question to source from call notes**: "What were you using before? What would you go back to if we didn't exist?"

---

### Step 2: Unique Attributes

**The question**: What do we have or do that our competitive alternatives don't?

Filter: If a well-funded competitor could replicate this in 6 months, it's not a durable unique attribute — it's a temporary lead.

True unique attributes tend to be:
- Architecture decisions that create trade-offs competitors can't easily reverse
- Proprietary data or network effects
- Deep domain expertise built into the product
- Patent-protected capabilities
- Integrations or ecosystem relationships that took years to build

---

### Step 3: Value for Customers

**The question**: What does each unique attribute enable for the buyer?

Always link: **Attribute → Outcome**

Examples:
- "Real-time anomaly detection [attribute] → ops teams know about data issues before they reach the board [outcome]"
- "Automated lineage mapping [attribute] → new analysts understand data pipelines in hours instead of weeks [outcome]"

Never leave an attribute floating without its outcome. Attributes are features. Outcomes are why people buy.

---

### Step 4: Target Customers

**The question**: Who values these specific outcomes the most?

ICP is defined by who gets the MOST value — not who you want to sell to, not who you have sold to, and not the biggest TAM.

A tight ICP makes positioning sharper. A vague ICP produces "we serve everyone" messaging that resonates with no one.

ICP definition includes:
- Job title / function
- Company size and stage
- Industry vertical (if relevant)
- Specific situation that makes them a great fit (the "trigger")

---

### Step 5: Market Category

**The question**: What game are you playing? What mental model should the buyer bring to the evaluation?

Category choice is a strategic decision that shapes every other messaging decision. Three options:

| Category Type | When to Use | Risk |
|--------------|-------------|------|
| Existing category ("better X") | Buyers already understand the problem | You compete on features against established players |
| Resegmented ("X for Y") | You win a specific segment strongly | Limits TAM perception |
| New category ("we invented Z") | Your product doesn't fit existing boxes | Requires market education investment |

**The customer intuition test**: Ask 5 prospects to describe what you do in one sentence, unprompted. The category they use is the one you're actually in, regardless of what you say.

## Positioning Statement Template

```
For [specific buyer persona with trigger situation]
Who [specific pain or job to be done — use customer language]
[Product Name] is a [market category]
That [primary value — outcome language, not feature language]
Unlike [primary competitive alternative]
We [specific differentiator — something the alternative genuinely cannot match]
```

**Worked example**:
```
For VP of Operations at Series B+ SaaS companies scaling their data infrastructure
Who are flying blind on data quality and find out about pipeline failures only after
downstream teams are already affected
AcmeObserve is a data pipeline observability platform
That gives ops teams real-time awareness of data anomalies before they reach stakeholders
Unlike Fivetran (which moves data but doesn't monitor it) and manual SQL alerting
We automatically learn what "normal" looks like for your specific data and surface
deviations before they propagate — no rule-writing required
```

## Evidence Requirements

| Positioning Component | Minimum Evidence Standard |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Competitive alternatives | 3+ unprompted customer mentions |
| Unique attributes | Internal validation OK + 1 customer confirmation |
| Value for customers | 5+ customer verbatims describing this outcome |
| Target customers | Observed pattern across 5+ closed-won deals |
| Market category | Customer intuition test with 3+ prospects |
